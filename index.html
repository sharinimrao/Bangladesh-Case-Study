Two rivers,
one destination.

Nepal cut childhood stunting nearly in half over two decades. Bangladesh has made real progress too — then stalled. This is the story of what one country got right, and what the other can still borrow.

🏔️ Part One

Nepal didn't fix one thing. It fixed five.

Between the mid-1990s and 2016, Nepal reduced childhood stunting despite poverty, political instability, rugged terrain, and thin infrastructure. No single program did that. It took simultaneous progress on healthcare, education, sanitation, women's empowerment, and household income — each reinforcing the others.

🏥 Health systems

Policy gave health workers a mandate. Mothers responded.

Health care spending in Nepal rose sharply starting in the mid-1990s, paying for new facilities and a nationwide community health worker cadre. The 1991 National Health Policy gave that workforce something to execute against: evidence-based priorities for maternal and child health.

The result wasn't just more clinics — it was a shift in behavior. More mothers sought skilled care during pregnancy and delivery, and adopted new child care and feeding practices. Stunting fell 47% since 1995 as that behavior change compounded year over year.

📚 Education

A generation of girls stayed in school. That alone moved the numbers.

In the 1990s, Nepal faced dismal primary enrollment and youth literacy, especially for girls. The government responded by decentralizing and investing in education infrastructure. The 1992 Basic Primary Education Program (BPEP) built new schools, enrolled over a million more children, and — alongside donor support — closed the primary-school gender gap almost entirely.

The downstream effect on stunting is direct: more educated girls become parents later. Later childbearing lowers the risk of preterm birth, intrauterine growth restriction, infant mortality, and undernutrition in the next generation.

💧 Sanitation

Nepal stopped supplying toilets and started building demand for them.

Earlier sanitation efforts focused on supply — building toilets and hoping people would use them. Community-Led Total Sanitation (CLTS), an NGO-led approach, flipped that: it generated grassroots demand instead, and the shift was dramatic. Communities that were roughly three-quarters open-defecating became roughly three-quarters toilet-using.

That single behavioral shift cut transmission of diarrhea and pneumonia — two of the most direct drivers of stunted growth in early childhood. In 2011, Nepal layered on a Total Behavior Change approach, keeping CLTS's core demand-generation but adding targeted subsidies.

🌾 Poverty & household income

Money sent home bought better food.

Economic growth and a steady flow of remittances pulled Nepalis out of poverty and raised disposable household income — income that went toward more nutritious food. Labor migrants abroad sent home remittances worth about a quarter of national GDP, complementing increasingly pro-poor government policy.

That income translated into higher food security, better diet quality, and more health-seeking behavior — the same behaviors the health and education investments were also driving. The pieces reinforced each other rather than operating in isolation.

🍚 Part Two

Bangladesh has the playbook. It doesn't have the conditions.

Bangladesh cut stunting from roughly 60% in the mid-1990s to around 24% today — real, hard-won progress. But the rate of decline has slowed well below the pace needed to hit global targets, and the reasons trace back to the same five pillars that worked for Nepal: this time, each one is under strain.

Political stability

The 2024 ousting of Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina left the government rebuilding nutrition programs from scratch mid-transition — exactly when consistency matters most.

Food security

Inflation hit a record 14.1%, making micronutrient-dense diets — and in many households, food at all — harder to afford.

Health infrastructure

Under 1% of GDP goes to healthcare. The public system underserves rural and poor populations; the private sector is unregulated, with high out-of-pocket costs.

Foreign aid

Reduced international funding — including the U.S. withdrawal from the WHO, a major donor — has made nutrition program financing increasingly unreliable.

🥭 What can transfer

Three things Bangladesh can borrow — and one Nepal had that's harder to import.

✓

Demand-driven sanitation

CLTS-style programs that build local demand for toilets, rather than just supplying them, are not capital-intensive. Bangladesh already runs WASH initiatives — the Nepal model suggests doubling down on community-led demand generation specifically, not just infrastructure spend.

✓

A dedicated community health worker cadre

Nepal's 1991 policy succeeded partly because it gave health workers a clear, evidence-based mandate — not just more funding. Bangladesh's underserved rural public sector could see outsized returns from a similarly structured, mandate-driven workforce rather than only added spending.

✓

Keep girls in school, deliberately

Nepal's gender parity gains weren't incidental — they came from a named program (BPEP) with enrollment targets. Bangladesh's existing girls'-education initiatives could adopt the same explicit, tracked-target structure.

△

Political continuity

Nepal's multisectoral approach worked partly because it had roughly two uninterrupted decades to compound. Bangladesh's 2024 transition is a reminder that program design can be copied — political stability cannot be, and it may be the single largest variable separating the two trajectories.
